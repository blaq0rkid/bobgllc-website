## The BOBG Scoring System: The 5-Factor Fit Score

We use five distinct factors to evaluate an agency’s suitability for your specific firm. Each factor is scored on a scale of 1 to 5. 

*   **Total 18–25:** High-probability target. Allocate maximum resources.
*   **Total 12–17:** Conditional target. Only pursue if you have an unfair advantage.
*   **Below 12:** Walk away. Do not pass go. Do not spend $1 on the bid.

### Factor 1: Mission Alignment
Does your core offer map to the agency’s current priorities for FY26–27? 

Every agency has a public-facing Strategic Plan. If you are targeting the Department of Veterans Affairs (VA), are you aligning with their current focus on electronic health record modernization or veteran suicide prevention? 

*   **Score 5:** Your services are mission-critical and mentioned in the current FY budget line items.
*   **Score 1:** Your services are "nice to have" or the agency is currently facing budget cuts in your category.

### Factor 2: Procurement History
Has the agency actually purchased what you sell in the last 24 months? 

Don’t listen to what Contracting Officers (KOs) say at Industry Days. Look at what they *do* with their checkbooks. Federal buying is habitual. Agencies that buy IT services from small businesses today are likely to do it again tomorrow. 

*   **Score 5:** Multiple recent awards to firms with your profile.
*   **Score 1:** No evidence they have purchased your category in the last two years.

### Factor 3: Set-Aside Utilization
Does the agency treat set-asides like a growth engine or a checkbox?

Some agencies, like the DHA, have aggressive small business utilization goals and a history of meeting them. Others pay lip service to the requirements but favor large incumbents through "bridge contracts" or complex requirements that only a prime can meet.

*   **Score 5:** Strong small-business utilization (35%+) and frequent use of your specific socio-economic status (SDVOSB, WOSB, 8(a)).
*   **Score 1:** Low utilization and heavy reliance on "incumbent lock-in."

![A precision compass on a map illustrating the 5-factor fit score for federal agency alignment.](https://cdn.marblism.com/RyicdWFeAlv.webp)

### Factor 4: Incumbent Vulnerability
Is the incumbent underperforming or nearing the end of their contract?

Replacing a tired incumbent is the most profitable way to grow. We look for "dissatisfaction signals": GAO protests, negative news cycles, or contracts that have been extended multiple times without a recompete.

*   **Score 5:** The contract is nearing a recompete and the incumbent has visible performance issues.
*   **Score 1:** The incumbent is high-performing, well-entrenched, and has a decade of history with the program office.

### Factor 5: Relationship Access
Do you have access to the people who shape the requirements?

Paper doesn’t beat proximity. If you only see a requirement when it hits SAM.gov, you are already too late. You need to be in the room during the "Pre-Solicitation" phase when the Statement of Work (SOW) is being drafted.

*   **Score 5:** You have existing relationships with the program office or the KO.
*   **Score 1:** Pure cold outreach. You are just a name on a capability statement.

## Actionable Intelligence: The 10-Minute Validation

You can start using this logic today without a complex CRM. Follow these steps on SAM.gov to validate your current targets:

1.  Go to **SAM.gov** and navigate to **Contract Data** (formerly FPDS reports).
2.  Run a "Static Report" or use the "Ad Hoc" tool.
3.  Filter by your primary **NAICS code** and your **Target Agency ID**.
4.  Set the date range for the **last 24 months**.
5.  **Analyze the results:** If the search returns "zero records" or only shows awards to multi-billion dollar primes, your "Factor 2" score is a 1. 

If you find that your "dream agency" hasn't actually spent money in your category for two years, stop chasing them. You are fighting for a market that doesn't exist. You can read more about this [reality check here](https://bobgllc.com/reality-check).
